[cite_start]This course introduces key concepts, legal categories, and empirical methods in migration economics, with a strict emphasis on evaluating empirical research and causal identification in labor markets[cite: 1045, 1046]. 

Course Overview
======
[cite_start]Designed for a capped seminar of 20 bachelor students[cite: 1041], the curriculum strips away standard textbook fluff to focus directly on empirical design. [cite_start]The course bridges foundational economic theory with modern applied microeconometrics, training students to scrutinize identification strategies—specifically Difference-in-Differences, Shift-Share IVs, and Synthetic Controls—rather than passively consuming published results[cite: 618, 619, 1051].

Structure
======
The course is structured to move rapidly from theory to applied critique:
* [cite_start]**Foundational Lectures:** Three intensive sessions covering the decision to migrate (selection and networks), human capital assimilation, and the supply-demand mechanics of spatial labor shocks[cite: 1050, 1054].
* [cite_start]**Applied Workshop (The Mariel Boatlift):** A hands-on case study dissecting the conflicting literature on the 1980 Mariel Boatlift[cite: 1055, 1064]. [cite_start]We rigorously compare Card (1990), Borjas (2017), Peri & Yasenov (2019), and Clemens & Hunt (2019) to illustrate how minor sample restrictions, small-cell compositional shifts, and control group choices fundamentally alter headline elasticities[cite: 1065, 1066].
* [cite_start]**Presentation Sessions:** Ten seminar sessions where students must defend an empirical paper from a curated reading list[cite: 1056, 1104]. [cite_start]Presentations are evaluated on the student's ability to clearly isolate the causal margin, explicitly map the identifying variation, and rigorously test threats to validity[cite: 842, 844].

Core Topics & Mechanisms
======
* [cite_start]**Selection & Sorting:** Positive versus negative selection via the Roy model, and how bilateral resistance and policy frictions shift moving costs[cite: 411, 418, 427].
* [cite_start]**Earnings Assimilation:** Reconciling Chiswick's cross-sectional human capital profiles with Borjas's cohort-effect critique[cite: 499].
* [cite_start]**Labor Market Adjustments:** Moving beyond partial equilibrium wage impacts to evaluate general equilibrium adjustments, including native mobility, capital deepening, and occupational upgrading[cite: 635, 636].
